The 6 Core NASW Values

Service

Prioritize serving others above self-interest; use skills to help people in need and address social problems.

Social Justice

Challenge injustice; pursue change on behalf of vulnerable and oppressed populations.

Dignity & Worth of the Person

Respect the inherent dignity of every person; mindful of individual differences.

Importance of Human Relationships

Relationships between people are essential vehicles for change; engage people as partners.

Integrity

Behave in a trustworthy manner; practice honestly and responsibly.

Competence

Continually develop professional expertise; practice only within areas of competence.

Exam tip: "Empathy" and "Altruism" are NOT official NASW core values — common trick questions!

Flexner's 6 Criteria for a Profession (1915)

1. Intellectual Operations

Work involves individual responsibility and creative thinking, not routine tasks.

2. Specialized Knowledge

Derives from science and learning; creates a distinct body of knowledge.

3. Teachable Techniques

Methods can be systematically communicated through professional education.

4. Practical Application

Knowledge serves definite, practical purposes in the real world.

5. Self-Organization

Professional associations set standards, share knowledge, and maintain quality.

6. Altruistic Motivation

Practitioners prioritize social service over financial gain.

Which historical root of social work is most directly connected to modern clinical/case management practice?

  • A Jane Addams & the Settlement House Movement
  • B Mary Richmond & the Charity Organization Societies (COS)
  • C Black mutual aid networks and institutional churches
  • D The Progressive Era labor movement
EXPLANATION Mary Richmond formalized the assess–diagnose–treat model in Social Diagnosis (1917), which became the foundation of clinical social work. The COS used "friendly visitors" to investigate individual needs and determine moral worthiness — this is the direct ancestor of today's micro/clinical practice. Jane Addams is most linked to macro/structural practice.

Which of the following is NOT one of the six core NASW values?

  • A Service
  • B Integrity
  • C Empathy
  • D Social Justice
EXPLANATION The six NASW core values are: Service, Social Justice, Dignity & Worth of the Person, Importance of Human Relationships, Integrity, and Competence. "Empathy" — though a critical social work skill — is not listed as a core value. "Altruism" is also commonly confused but is not an official NASW value.
3

John Rawls's "Difference Principle" states that social and economic inequalities are just ONLY if they:

  • A Are the result of individual choices and hard work
  • B Are distributed equally among all citizens
  • C Benefit the least advantaged members of society
  • D Are approved by a democratic majority
EXPLANATION Rawls argued that while perfect equality isn't required, any inequality that exists must work to benefit those who are worst off in society. His Principle of Redress also holds that undeserved inequalities (based on birth, luck, natural endowment) should be compensated. This is directly applicable to social work: social workers prioritize the most vulnerable.
4

In the EBP evidence hierarchy, which type of study provides the STRONGEST evidence for an intervention's effectiveness?

  • A Expert opinion and practice wisdom
  • B Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs)
  • C Systematic reviews and meta-analyses
  • D Qualitative case studies
EXPLANATION Systematic reviews and meta-analyses sit at the top of the evidence hierarchy because they synthesize findings from multiple high-quality studies, producing a more reliable overall picture than any single study. RCTs are strong individually ("gold standard"), but systematic reviews of multiple RCTs are even stronger. Expert opinion is at the bottom.
5

The "Person-in-Environment" (PIE) framework emphasizes that social workers should:

  • A Focus exclusively on changing individual behaviors and thought patterns
  • B Prioritize policy change over direct client services
  • C Understand clients within their multiple, nested social contexts — including family, community, and structural systems
  • D Assess only the immediate presenting problem before intervening
EXPLANATION PIE is one of social work's most distinctive contributions. Rather than seeing people as isolated individuals to "fix," PIE situates every person within multiple, nested contexts (micro, mezzo, macro). Effective practice requires attention to all these levels — recognizing how environmental forces create and contribute to problems in living.
6

According to Abraham Flexner (1915), what was his primary criticism of social work as a profession?

  • A Social workers were too focused on community organizing rather than individual care
  • B Social workers lacked altruistic motivation
  • C Social work lacked a distinct, specialized body of scientific knowledge and technique
  • D Social workers had no professional associations
EXPLANATION Flexner's core concern was that social work didn't generate its own distinctive knowledge base — it largely borrowed from psychology, sociology, and medicine. He acknowledged social work's altruism and organization but questioned whether it had the intellectual rigor and specialized technique of established professions. Morris (2008) later argued that Flexner was misread — he valued evolving intellectual judgment, not just positivist science.
7

Community organizing differs from case advocacy primarily because community organizing:

  • A Focuses on solving problems for individual clients
  • B Is led by professional social workers who identify community needs
  • C Builds collective power among affected people to demand change, with those affected leading the effort
  • D Works through legislative lobbying and testimony alone
EXPLANATION The key distinction in community organizing is collective action BY those directly affected. Unlike case advocacy (which solves an individual's problem) or policy advocacy (which may be elite-driven), community organizing builds a base of affected people, identifies targets and pressure points, and shifts power to the community itself. The social worker facilitates — they do NOT lead the agenda.
8

Martha Nussbaum's Capabilities Approach differs from Rawls's theory of justice primarily because Nussbaum focuses on:

  • A Designing fair institutional rules and procedures
  • B What people are actually able to do and be — ensuring a minimum threshold of human capabilities
  • C Maximizing overall happiness for the greatest number of people
  • D Distributing equal income to all citizens regardless of need
EXPLANATION Rawls focuses on fair procedures and institutions (who gets what resources through just rules). Nussbaum shifts to asking what people can actually do and become — two people with the same income may have vastly different capabilities if one has a disability requiring costly care. Justice, for Nussbaum, requires that every person reach a minimum threshold in each of her 10 central human capabilities.
9

What is a key tension that arose from social work's effort to "professionalize" after Flexner's 1915 critique?

  • A Social workers became too radical and community-oriented
  • B The push for scientific legitimacy sometimes came at the cost of social work's grassroots, reform-oriented identity
  • C Licensure requirements made social work too accessible to diverse practitioners
  • D The profession lost its focus on individual casework
EXPLANATION After Flexner, social work pursued legitimacy through standardized education and psychoanalytic frameworks — often at the expense of its settlement house, reform-oriented roots. Brekke (2012) argued social work still "piggybacks" on other disciplines. The course frames this as an ongoing tension: does becoming more "scientific" narrow social work's moral and political commitments?
10

According to Hounmenou (2012), Black settlement houses served as "free spaces" because:

  • A They operated without any funding constraints or white philanthropic oversight
  • B They were explicitly endorsed by mainstream white social work organizations
  • C Racial segregation paradoxically created spaces for collective identity, shared reflection, and the development of oppositional consciousness
  • D They focused only on providing services, not on political advocacy
EXPLANATION Hounmenou's key insight is that despite being constrained by white philanthropic oversight and respectability politics, the segregated nature of these institutions created limited surveillance, cultural safety, and shared experiences of oppression that allowed oppositional consciousness to emerge. Segregation — though oppressive — paradoxically created the conditions for political resistance.
11

In Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), the central assumption is that:

  • A Problems are primarily rooted in unconscious childhood experiences
  • B Problems are often rooted in irrational or dysfunctional thinking; thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are interrelated
  • C Poverty and systemic inequality are the root cause of most mental health struggles
  • D The client's social network is the primary driver of behavior change
EXPLANATION CBT theory (Beck, 2020) posits that cognitions (thoughts), feelings (emotions), and behaviors are all interrelated and mutually influential. Irrational or dysfunctional thinking leads to problems; by identifying and changing those thought patterns, clients can change feelings and behaviors. CBT is used widely across populations and is considered evidence-based for depression, anxiety, and trauma.
12

Wagner's (1989) three-stage cycle of radical social work movements ends with:

  • A Client-led militancy as the dominant form of practice
  • B Complete overthrow of the professional establishment
  • C Absorption — radical ideas are partially incorporated into mainstream norms, often losing their transformative edge
  • D Permanent institutionalization of radical practice frameworks
EXPLANATION Wagner's three stages: (1) Client-led militancy — oppressed groups radicalize social workers; (2) Militant professionalism — radicals challenge professional leadership from within; (3) Absorption — radical ideas get incorporated into mainstream norms but lose their transformative edge. This cycle repeats. The question "where is social work in this cycle right now?" is a key course discussion prompt.

Week 2 · History

Compare the Charity Organization Society (COS) approach to poverty with the Settlement House Movement approach. How do they reflect different understandings of what causes poverty?

Aim for 4–6 sentences using course vocabulary
MODEL ANSWER The COS approach, associated with Mary Richmond and formalized in Social Diagnosis (1917), understood poverty as primarily resulting from individual moral failings — laziness, intemperance, or poor choices. Their method was to assess individual "worthiness," provide casework, and promote self-sufficiency through personal reform. In contrast, Jane Addams and the Settlement House Movement understood poverty as structural — produced by industrialization, exploitative labor conditions, and unjust systems. Settlement house workers lived alongside immigrant communities, provided education and childcare, but also organized labor unions and advocated for child labor laws and tenement reform. The fundamental difference is the unit of analysis: COS targeted individual character (micro practice), while Settlement Houses targeted neighborhood conditions and policy systems (macro practice). Both traditions persist in social work today, reflecting the ongoing tension between social control and social liberation.
Week 3 · Professionalization

What are two tensions introduced by the "professionalization" of social work that persist today? Use specific examples.

Name and explain two distinct tensions
MODEL ANSWER Tension 1 — Gatekeeping vs. Access: Licensing and credentialing protect the public by ensuring minimum competency, but also create barriers that can exclude talented practitioners from low-income backgrounds or communities of color. The cost of accredited MSW programs, licensing exams, and supervised hours can limit who enters the profession.

Tension 2 — Scientific Legitimacy vs. Community Knowledge: Evidence-based practice improves outcomes, but "valid" research has historically excluded Indigenous knowledge, immigrant wisdom, and community-tested strategies. Brekke (2012) argued social work still borrows its scientific legitimacy from psychology and sociology, rather than producing its own distinctive knowledge — meaning the profession may privilege quantitative research over lived experience and practice wisdom. Both tensions reflect the broader question: in trying to become more "professional," what does social work risk losing?
Week 4 · Ethics

Explain what makes an "ethical dilemma" in social work. Using the Mr. X case (elderly client refusing help while showing cognitive decline), identify the specific dilemma and name the competing NASW standards involved.

Name the specific NASW standards by number or title
MODEL ANSWER An ethical dilemma occurs when a practitioner faces two or more competing ethical principles, where honoring one value appears to require compromising another. Unlike a clear violation, a dilemma involves tension between two defensible "rights."

In the Mr. X case, the dilemma is between: Standard 1.02 (Self-Determination) — social workers respect and promote clients' right to direct their own lives; Mr. X has clearly stated he wants to remain independent; and Standard 1.01 (Commitment to Client Well-Being) — the primary responsibility is to promote client well-being, which may be limited when clients' actions pose serious, foreseeable harm to themselves. Both principles are ethically sound. The challenge isn't choosing right over wrong — it's determining how to honor both when they pull in opposite directions. A good ethical decision-making process involves gathering facts, consulting the code, considering duty-based vs. consequence-based reasoning, exploring biases, and deciding what one can justify to the client, supervisor, and oneself.
Week 5 · Social Justice

Using Rawls's Difference Principle, explain whether the racial wealth gap in the United States constitutes a social injustice. Be specific about what the principle requires.

Apply Rawls directly — what specifically fails his test?
MODEL ANSWER Rawls's Difference Principle holds that social and economic inequalities are just only if they benefit the least advantaged members of society. His Principle of Redress adds that inequalities based on undeserved factors (birth, race, luck) should be compensated by society. Applied to the racial wealth gap — where the median white family holds roughly 10x the wealth of the median Black family — this gap fails Rawls's test on multiple counts: First, the inequality does not benefit the least advantaged; it actively concentrates resources among already advantaged groups. Second, the gap is produced by undeserved structural factors: historical exclusion from homeownership (redlining), discriminatory lending, and intergenerational wealth denial — not individual choice. Third, the conditions for fair equality of opportunity (Rawls's second principle) were not and are not met. By Rawlsian standards, the racial wealth gap is an injustice that demands redress through compensatory policies.
Week 6 · Multi-Level Practice

Explain why multi-level practice is essential in social work, using a specific example to show how micro, mezzo, and macro levels interact.

Show how one level affects the others — use a case example
MODEL ANSWER Multi-level practice recognizes that individual struggles often reflect systemic failures, and that sustainable change requires simultaneous intervention at individual, organizational, and systemic levels. Consider Maya (15-year-old from the school case): at the micro level, she needs counseling for depression and family stress management. At the mezzo level, the school's truancy system is sending threatening letters without understanding her structural constraints — organizational policy reform is needed. At the macro level, the subsidized childcare center closed due to state funding cuts, and the bus route serving their neighborhood was eliminated — these are policy failures requiring advocacy and systemic reform. Intervening only at the micro level (counseling Maya) does nothing about the childcare gap driving her absences, and does nothing about the transit policy. Multi-level thinking reveals root causes that single-level analysis misses and produces more durable change.
Week 9 · Macro Practice

What is the social worker's role in community organizing, and how does it differ from the role in direct service? Why does Zelnick et al. argue that "our jobs aren't apolitical"?

Address the organizer's role AND the political argument
MODEL ANSWER In community organizing, the social worker's role is fundamentally different from direct service: rather than providing help to individuals, the organizer facilitates collective action by the affected community itself. The organizer identifies natural leaders, builds relationships through one-on-one conversations, conducts power analysis (who can turn "No" into "Yes"?), helps strategize pressure tactics, and asks powerful questions — but does NOT decide the agenda, take credit for victories, or speak for the community. This represents a shift from "helping" to "liberating."

Zelnick et al. (2022) argue that social workers are often the first to witness systemic failures in clients' lives — inadequate housing, food insecurity, discriminatory policies — that therapy alone cannot address. When social workers see these patterns, their ethical obligation extends beyond the therapy room. Refusing to act on structural injustice is itself a political choice that maintains the status quo. The tension they name is working within the "Nonprofit Industrial Complex" — institutions that claim to serve communities but may maintain control over them — while still organizing for liberation.

Vignette 1: Rosa Medina — Housing, Displacement & Multi-Level Practice

★ This case was used in the actual midterm review sessions
Client Scenario

Rosa Medina is a 38-year-old Latina woman living in a mid-sized U.S. city. She was the first in her family to attend college, earning an associate's degree in business. For twelve years she worked as an office manager at a manufacturing plant. Three years ago, the plant permanently closed, eliminating Rosa's job along with 400 others. Unemployment benefits ran out after six months. Since then she has worked part-time jobs — cashier, home health aide, delivery driver — none offering benefits, consistent hours, or wages above $14/hr.


Last year, Rosa's landlord raised her rent from $950 to $1,400/month. After three months of late payments and one eviction notice, Rosa moved her two teenage children into her sister's two-bedroom apartment. Five people share the space; Rosa sleeps on the couch.


Rosa has been referred to the Southside Family Resource Center after her 15-year-old son Diego showed frequent absences and withdrawal at school. During intake, Rosa appears exhausted and says: "I've worked my whole life. I don't want to be a burden to anyone." She reports significant anxiety, hasn't seen a doctor in two years (lost health insurance when plant closed), is behind on car payments, and has not enrolled in any public assistance programs because she didn't know she might be eligible and felt ashamed to ask.

Apply the Frameworks — Answer Each Question

1. Using the Person-in-Environment (PIE) framework, identify at least three structural/systemic factors (not personal failings) contributing to Rosa's situation. Which populations do these structures disproportionately affect?

MODEL ANSWER Structural factors (PIE lens):
• Housing instability / near-homelessness — produced by market-rate rent increases and lack of tenant protections
• Job displacement and wage stagnation — manufacturing closures eliminate stable, working-class employment
• Lack of healthcare access — employment-based insurance system leaves workers uninsured when jobs disappear
• Child welfare concerns — Diego's school struggles are downstream of housing instability, not a behavioral problem
• Benefit system barriers — complex eligibility rules and stigma prevent qualified people from accessing support

Disproportionately affected populations: Latina women (higher rates of housing cost burden), working-class communities (hit hardest by manufacturing closures), low-income adults (highest uninsured rates), children in unstable housing (higher rates of school absence and anxiety). This is NOT individual failure — these are structural patterns.

2. As a micro-level social worker (case manager), what are your IMMEDIATE priorities upon meeting Rosa? What roles would you perform? Apply the 7-step direct practice process.

MODEL ANSWER Engagement: Build rapport with genuine warmth; acknowledge Rosa's hard work and the structural injustice of her situation. Do not pathologize her shame — contextualize it as a normal response to stigma around poverty.

Assessment (biopsychosocial): Understand all dimensions of her situation — housing, income, health, mental health, children's needs, social support. Use the PHQ-9 to screen for depression. Conduct PIE assessment of barriers and strengths.

Case Manager roles:
• Resource broker: Connect Rosa to SNAP/EBT, Medi-Cal, America's Job Centers, emergency rental assistance, school support for Diego
• Care coordinator: Coordinate referrals across service providers
• Case advocate: Defend Rosa's right to benefits, advocate for fair housing, connect Diego to school counseling and tutoring
• People changer (if licensed): CBT for anxiety/depression; family therapy

Planning: Collaboratively set SMART goals — e.g., "Apply for Medi-Cal within 2 weeks"; "Meet with school counselor about Diego by [date]"

3. Apply Rawls's Difference Principle to Rosa's case. What specific inequalities exist? Do they benefit the least advantaged? What would Rawls say justice requires?

MODEL ANSWER Rawls's Difference Principle states inequalities are just only if they benefit the least advantaged. Applied to Rosa's case:

The inequalities present — wage stagnation, the absence of portable healthcare, weak tenant protections, and a safety net that Rosa couldn't easily navigate — do NOT benefit the least advantaged; they actively harm them. Rosa's struggles stem not from personal failings but from undeserved structural disadvantages: the plant closure (not her choice), a system that ties healthcare to employment, a housing market without adequate protections, and benefits that require navigation of complex, stigmatizing bureaucracy.

Rawls's Principle of Redress further requires that undeserved disadvantages be compensated. His verdict: Rosa's situation is unjust. Justice would require: living wages and portable benefits, affordable housing policies, accessible healthcare regardless of employment status, and safety net systems designed to be navigable without shame. Social workers embody the Difference Principle by prioritizing clients like Rosa and advocating for these structural changes.

4. What would a MACRO-level social worker do to address the root causes producing situations like Rosa's? Describe at least two macro-level roles and specific actions.

MODEL ANSWER Policy Advocate: Lobby legislators for expanded SNAP eligibility, childcare subsidies, and tenant protection laws. Advocate for healthcare decoupled from employment (Medicaid expansion). Testify at city council hearings about the impact of rent increases on working families. Document how manufacturing closures without worker safety nets create downstream crises for families of color.

Community Organizer: Build coalitions with other displaced manufacturing workers and Latina families facing housing insecurity. Conduct power analysis — who controls housing policy in this city? Who can make affordable housing happen? Help affected families organize collectively to pressure housing authorities and landlords. Center the voices of those affected; don't speak for them.

Program Developer: Create bilingual job training programs and culturally adapted mental health services that specifically address the barriers Latina women face (language, immigration status concerns, cultural stigma around mental health).

All of these shrink the scope of the underlying problem — they don't just address Rosa's individual symptoms, they target root causes.

Vignette 2: Maya — School Social Work & Systems-Ecological Practice

Week 6 Case Study
Client Scenario

Maya is a 15-year-old sophomore who, until recently, was an honors student with a 3.8 GPA passionate about the debate team. Over the last six weeks, her attendance has plummeted — she has missed 12 days and is failing Algebra II. The school's automated truancy system triggered a formal warning letter citing potential legal intervention.


Maya lives with her mother Elena (who works two hourly jobs) and 4-year-old brother Leo. Two months ago, the subsidized childcare center Leo attended closed due to loss of state funding. Elena now relies on Maya to stay home during the gap hours between her shifts. The family lives in a transit desert — the city bus route Maya used was recently eliminated due to municipal budget cuts, and the school also canceled its Late Bus program.


When meeting the school social worker, Maya appears exhausted with frequent headaches. She says: "I can't leave my brother alone, and my mom can't lose her job or we'll be evicted." She has stopped responding to friends because she feels "embarrassed" and "like a failure." She wants to go to college but now feels it is "impossible."

Apply the Frameworks

1. Map the systems: Identify at least three factors at EACH level (micro, mezzo, macro) affecting Maya's situation.

MODEL ANSWER Micro (individual/family): Maya's exhaustion and headaches, depression/shame, loss of social connections; Elena's precarious employment and housing stress; Leo's lack of childcare; family communication under severe stress

Mezzo (organizations/community): School's automated truancy system sending threatening letters without understanding context; school cancellation of Late Bus program; absence of after-school resources; no alternative childcare agency in the area

Macro (systems/policy): State funding cuts eliminating the subsidized childcare center; municipal budget cuts eliminating the bus route; housing market producing eviction threat; policy design that punishes rather than supports families in crisis; lack of adequate working-family support systems

Key interaction: The macro-level childcare funding cut created a mezzo-level gap (no childcare center), which created a micro-level reality (Maya must stay home), which the school system treated as an individual behavioral problem. Without a systems lens, Maya gets punished; with it, social workers can intervene at all levels.

2. As Maya's school social worker, what is one micro, one mezzo, and one macro intervention you would pursue? Connect each to NASW core values.

MODEL ANSWER Micro: Provide individual counseling to help Maya process shame and rebuild her sense of self-efficacy. Use CBT to challenge the thought pattern that she is "a failure" and to separate her worth from her circumstances. NASW Value: Dignity & Worth of the Person — respect Maya's inherent worth and help her reconnect with her goals.

Mezzo: Advocate within the school to halt or pause the truancy warning letter; meet with administration to explain the structural context and prevent punitive measures. Propose a modified attendance plan that accommodates Maya's caregiving responsibilities. NASW Value: Social Justice — challenge institutional processes that harm vulnerable students.

Macro: Document this case and others like it to build evidence for a policy proposal to restore after-school childcare subsidies or emergency transportation funds. Join a coalition advocating for working-family policies at the city or state level. NASW Value: Service — using professional knowledge to address root causes, not just symptoms.

Vignette 3: Maya (29) — Evidence-Based Practice & Cultural Competence

Week 8 Case Study — EBP in Practice
Client Scenario

Maya is a 29-year-old first-time mother who immigrated from a collectivist culture five years ago. She is six months postpartum and reports persistent feelings of worthlessness, extreme fatigue, and a "frightening" lack of connection to her infant. Her primary care physician recommended antidepressants, but Maya is breastfeeding and deeply concerned about the "shame" of medication in her community. She wants a "natural" or talk-based solution but feels overwhelmed by options. She is culturally isolated, with limited family support in the U.S.

Apply the EBP Framework

1. Walk through the 5-step EBP process for Maya's case. What question would you "Ask"? What would you look for when you "Appraise" the evidence?

MODEL ANSWER Ask: "For a postpartum immigrant woman from a collectivist culture who refuses medication and is breastfeeding, what non-pharmacological interventions are most effective for postpartum depression?"

Acquire: Search Cochrane Library, Campbell Collaboration for systematic reviews on postpartum depression; look specifically for studies including immigrant women and culturally adapted CBT.

Appraise: Evaluate (1) strength of evidence — systematic review > RCTs > quasi-experimental; (2) applicability — were women from collectivist cultures included in the sample? If the "gold standard" evidence is from white, Western, middle-class populations, its generalizability to Maya is limited; (3) cultural fit — does the intervention account for Maya's values, family structure, and cultural understanding of mental health?

Apply: Implement collaboratively — discuss options with Maya, involve her preferences and cultural framework. CBT adapted for postpartum depression may be appropriate; group therapy with other immigrant mothers may also reduce isolation.

Audit: Use PHQ-9 or Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale at intervals to measure outcomes; adjust plan based on Maya's feedback and changing circumstances.

2. The Cochrane Library indicates that medication + CBT is the "gold standard" for postpartum depression, but Maya refuses medication. Is it ethical to proceed with a "lesser" evidence-based option? Explain using NASW values.

MODEL ANSWER Yes — it is both ethical and appropriate to proceed with a non-medication intervention when a client refuses medication for informed, culturally grounded reasons. Here's why:

Self-Determination (Standard 1.02): Social workers respect and promote clients' right to direct their own lives. Maya's refusal is not irrational — it is informed by her cultural context, breastfeeding concerns, and community relationships. Overriding her stated wishes to apply the "gold standard" would violate this core standard.

Cultural Competence (Standard 1.05): Social workers must understand culture and its function in human behavior. In Maya's cultural context, the shame associated with psychiatric medication is a real barrier that affects treatment engagement. Ignoring it doesn't make it disappear — it makes Maya less likely to engage with any treatment.

Dignity & Worth: The three pillars of EBP are research evidence, client values and preferences, AND clinical expertise — all three must be balanced. A "weaker" evidence-based intervention that Maya will actually engage with is more effective than a "stronger" one she will refuse. EBP is a tool to enhance judgment, not replace it.

Vignette 4: East Oakland Food Justice — Community Organizing

Week 9 · Strategy Session Case
Community Scenario

You are a social worker at a Family Resource Center in East Oakland, at 66th Ave and International Blvd — a high-density neighborhood classified as a "food desert" with limited access to fresh, affordable produce. Many families rely on food banks and corner stores with processed foods. Residents want to convert part of an underutilized city park into a community garden. The Parks & Recreation Department said "No," citing maintenance, liability, and existing programming concerns. You want to help the community turn that "No" into a "Yes."

Apply the Organizing Framework

1. Conduct a power analysis: Who is your primary target (who can actually make this decision)? Who are secondary targets? What might be their vulnerabilities or pressure points?

MODEL ANSWER Primary target: The Parks & Recreation Department Director (or City Council member who oversees the department) — someone with actual authority to reverse the "No," not the staff person who said it.

Secondary targets: City Council member(s) representing the district; the Mayor (who oversees city departments); budget committee members (parks funding flows through them); community allies who can apply political pressure.

Leverage points / vulnerabilities:
• Reputation: Public officials care about media coverage; "City blocks food garden for food-insecure families" is a bad headline.
• Elections: Council members are accountable to voters; organized constituents showing up at town halls and voting against incumbents is real pressure.
• Budget hearings: If the community can show up en masse at budget hearings, it demonstrates political will.
• Existing city policies: Does the city have food security or equity commitments? If so, that's leverage — the refusal contradicts stated policy.
• Liability concerns can often be addressed through community management agreements — this may be a negotiating opening.

2. What is your role as the social worker in this campaign? What should you NOT do, and what SHOULD you do? Connect to the ethical tensions in organizing.

MODEL ANSWER What you should NOT do:
• Do not decide the strategy or tactics for the community — that disempowers them and replicates the power dynamic you're trying to challenge.
• Do not speak for community members in public settings without their explicit consent.
• Do not expose community members to retaliation risks without their informed consent.
• Do not take credit for victories — this is their campaign.

What you SHOULD do:
• Build relationships through one-on-one conversations with residents to understand their stake in the issue.
• Identify and support natural community leaders — who already has trust and credibility?
• Provide training and resources (power analysis training, meeting facilitation, media literacy).
• Ask powerful questions that help the community understand the power dynamics.

Ethical tensions:
• Boundary confusion: Your agency role (resource provider) may conflict with your organizing role (pressure-campaign supporter).
• Confidentiality: When personal stories enter public action, you must ensure explicit consent.
• Nonprofit industrial complex (Zelnick): Your paycheck may come from institutions that are themselves invested in maintaining the status quo — this is a real tension to navigate honestly.

Vignette 5: Mr. X — Ethical Dilemma in Elder Care

Week 4 · Ethical Decision-Making
Client Scenario

You are a social worker at a senior housing complex. Mr. X, an 82-year-old resident, lives independently and has done so for 15 years. Over the past six months, neighbors report him leaving the stove on, forgetting medications, difficulty with hygiene, wandering outside at night disoriented, and a recent fall causing minor injury. When you meet with him to discuss concerns, he is adamant: "This is my home. I've lived here 15 years. I'm fine." He refuses in-home care or assisted living. The facility director is pressuring you to "do something." Family members out of state have mixed opinions.

Navigate the Ethical Dilemma

1. Name the ethical dilemma precisely. What two NASW standards are in tension? Use the language of the Code of Ethics.

MODEL ANSWER The dilemma: tension between Standard 1.02 (Self-Determination) — social workers respect and promote the right of clients to self-determination, including the right to make decisions about their own lives — and Standard 1.01 (Commitment to Client Well-Being) — social workers' primary responsibility is to promote client well-being, which may be limited when clients' actions pose serious, foreseeable harm to themselves or others.

This is a genuine dilemma because BOTH principles are ethically sound. It is not a question of right vs. wrong — it is a question of how to honor both values when they pull in opposite directions. Mr. X has a right to decide how he lives; the social worker has an obligation to protect his well-being. There is no formula — ethical reasoning, not rule-following, is required.

2. Apply Gewirth's hierarchy and Dolgoff et al.'s principle hierarchy to this case. What do they suggest about which value should take priority?

MODEL ANSWER Gewirth's hierarchy: Basic goods (life, safety, health) sit at the top — they are prerequisites for any action at all. Non-subtractive goods (autonomy, rights) are important but secondary. Additive goods (quality of life, self-esteem) come last. Applied here: Mr. X's physical safety (wandering at night, stove left on, falls) represents a basic goods concern that is higher in the hierarchy than his autonomy (a non-subtractive good).

Dolgoff et al.'s hierarchy: (1) Protection of life is highest; (3) Autonomy and freedom is third; (5) Quality of life is fifth. This also suggests that if there is genuine risk to Mr. X's life, that concern ranks above his autonomy.

However: these hierarchies are tools for reasoning, not automatic answers. The social worker still must assess: How imminent is the danger? Has Mr. X been assessed for cognitive capacity? Are there harm-reduction alternatives that preserve autonomy while addressing safety? (e.g., medication management, stove shut-off device, check-in system) The hierarchy guides, but professional judgment, additional information, and creative alternatives must also be considered.

Duty-Based vs. Consequence-Based Ethics

Deontological (Duty-Based)

Focus: Principles, rights, inherent duties

Key Question: What does this person have a right to? What is my obligation regardless of outcome?

Example: "Mr. X has a fundamental right to self-determination. Even if the outcome might be harmful, I must respect his autonomy."

Philosophers: Kant, W.D. Ross

Teleological (Consequence-Based)

Focus: Outcomes, harms, benefits, results

Key Question: What action will produce the best overall outcome or minimize harm?

Example: "If we allow Mr. X to live unsupervised, the likely outcome is serious injury. Restricting autonomy prevents greater harm."

Philosophers: Mill (Utilitarianism)

Quick Ethics Practice Questions

E1

An ethical dilemma is best described as a situation where:

  • A A social worker knows the right answer but doesn't want to implement it
  • B A client is clearly engaging in illegal behavior
  • C Two or more legitimate ethical principles are in conflict, where honoring one requires compromising another
  • D A social worker disagrees with a supervisor's decision
EXPLANATION Real ethical dilemmas involve genuine tension between two "rights" — not an obvious choice between right and wrong. The word "dilemma" (from Greek: "two horns") captures this: you are caught between two difficult choices, and either direction has ethical weight. If the answer is obvious, it's not truly a dilemma.
E2

The NABSW (National Association of Black Social Workers) Code of Ethics is notable because it:

  • A Focuses exclusively on clinical therapy techniques
  • B Explicitly centers racial justice, stating that the primary obligation is the welfare of the Black individual, family, and community
  • C Applies only to social workers working in government agencies
  • D Replaces the NASW Code for Black social workers
EXPLANATION The NABSW Code explicitly states that practitioners' "primary obligation is the welfare of the Black individual, Black family, and Black community." This demonstrates how centering specific communities and racial justice can shift what counts as "ethical" — foregrounding what might remain invisible in more general, universalist codes. The IFSW Global Code similarly centers human rights and social justice at the international level.

Social Justice Frameworks

WEEK 5 · RAWLS · SEN · NUSSBAUM · CARE
Key distinction: Social injustice (structural, group-based) ≠ individual injustice (one person wronging another). Social work focuses on social injustice — patterns built into institutions and systems that disadvantage entire groups based on identity, status, or circumstances beyond individual control.

Equality vs. Equity

Equality

Same resources for everyone. Treats people identically regardless of starting point.

May perpetuate existing disadvantages. Simpler to implement.

Equity

Resources adjusted to needs. Calibrated to help everyone reach fair outcomes, accounting for different starting points.

Recognizes different needs; aims for fair outcomes. Requires nuanced assessment.

Applying Frameworks: Is This a Social Injustice?

J1

A Rawlsian analysis of mass incarceration in the U.S. (which disproportionately affects Black and low-income communities) would conclude that:

  • A It is just because the criminal justice system applies the same laws to everyone equally
  • B It is unjust because it constitutes an undeserved disadvantage based on race and class that does not benefit the least advantaged, and fair equality of opportunity is not genuinely accessible
  • C It is a social problem but not a social injustice, since individuals chose to commit crimes
  • D It is just as long as basic liberties are formally guaranteed to all citizens
EXPLANATION Rawls tests whether inequalities benefit the least advantaged and whether access is genuinely fair. Mass incarceration fails both tests: (1) it does not benefit those most disadvantaged; it concentrates disadvantage further — removing people from communities, families, and employment. (2) The conditions producing incarceration (poverty, inadequate schools, discrimination in policing) mean that access to opportunity is not genuinely equal. Rawls's Principle of Redress would also require compensation for inequalities based on undeserved social position.
J2

Sen's Capabilities Approach would say that two people receiving the same monthly income are NOT equally situated if:

  • A One person lives in a more expensive city
  • B One person has a disability requiring expensive care, leaving them with fewer real capabilities despite equal income
  • C One person works longer hours to earn that income
  • D One person saves more of their income than the other
EXPLANATION Sen's core insight is that resources alone don't capture what people can actually do and be. Two people with identical incomes may have vastly different capabilities if one needs expensive medication or assistive devices. Justice requires looking at actual functioning and freedom — the real opportunity to achieve what one has reason to value — not just resource distribution.
J3

Care ethics (Noddings) differs most fundamentally from distributive justice frameworks (Rawls, Sen) by:

  • A Focusing on equal distribution of resources across society
  • B Emphasizing individual rights and liberties above all else
  • C Starting from human interdependence and relationships rather than rules or resource allocation; centering the moral value of caring work
  • D Applying only to private, family-based caregiving relationships
EXPLANATION Care ethics begins with the observation that humans are inherently interdependent — not autonomous individuals. It centers relationships, vulnerability, and the emotional/relational labor that sustains human life (often feminized and racialized). Distributive justice frameworks tend to assume independent, rational actors operating within institutions. Care ethics asks: who cares for whom, under what conditions, and how is that work valued? Combined with Social Reproduction Theory, it reveals that capitalism exploits the moral imperative to care.

The Individualist vs. Structuralist Distinction

Individualist Narrative

Places focus only on the individual — personal choice, responsibility, or misfortune. "She made bad decisions." "He should have worked harder."

When useful: understanding personal agency. When harmful: ignores structural barriers, pathologizes poverty.

Structuralist Narrative

Places focus on systemic factors — systemic racism, institutional inequality, structural barriers. "Housing policy produced this crisis."

Social work's distinctive contribution: structural competency — understanding how structures produce "where clients are."

Structural competency means we assess the historical, philosophical, ethical, political, and social implications of a client's situation — not just the immediate presenting problem. "Meet the client where they are" requires understanding structurally HOW they got there.
